Autonomous Vehicles

An autonomous vehicle, or as they are more commonly referred to as ‘driverless cars’, is a vehicle that is able to operate itself and perform necessary functions without any human intervention, through the ability to sense its surroundings. (TWI Ltd, 2019)

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) currently defines 6 levels of driving automation ranging from Level 0 (fully manual) to Level 5 (fully autonomous). Currently most Autonomous vehicles fall into Level 2 (Partial Driving Automation) due to the regulatory processes that allow the vehicles to be on the road, however manufacturers do have vehicles that are at a Level 3 (Conditional Driving Automation) which are in the process of being introduced into the market. (Synopsys, Inc., n.d.)

The latest technology to come from the Autonomous Vehicle field would be Tesla designing their own silicon chips to help with their artificial intelligence (AI). The AI chip, that they have called D1, has been designed to help train the machine-learning algorithm that is behind their Autopilot self-driving system. The D1 chip will then accelerate the implementation of the self-driving system, bringing Tesla closer to their goal of a Full Self-Driving (FSD) vehicle. (Knight, 2021)

The current technology used within autonomous vehicles that is marketable allows drivers to have a semi-autonomous driving experience. Tesla is a really great example of what is actively available to the everyday consumer. Their “Autopilot” system enables the vehicle to steer, accelerate and brake automatically within its lane. It will also suggest things like lane changes, recognise speed signs then lower the cruise control to suit, and can also ‘see’ things such as traffic lights, stop signs and roundabouts. The only thing the driver needs to do is interact with the steering wheel every 60 seconds, this allows the vehicle to know that the driver is still alert and keeping an eye on how it is operating. (Tesla, n.d.)

Within this decade it is anticipated that autonomous vehicles will become fully autonomous, in that there will be no need for driver interaction for it to continue driving. It is also predicted that one in every 10 vehicles on the road will be fully autonomous. Autonomous vehicles will also be on a 5G network (or the newest version of it) which will allow them to communicate with each

In the near future there will also be driverless taxi/ride share services. Currently Waymo has an autonomous ride-hailing service called ‘Waymo One’ that is offering fully autonomous rides in the East Valley of Phoenix, Arizona. They will be expanding to San Francisco as their next area for research and on from there

When looking at the technology behind autonomous vehicles, you can see that there are so many different components working together. The vehicles are equipped with things such as radar, cameras, ultrasound, and radio antennas. All these technologies combined help provide the autonomy of the vehicle, while also making it reliable. Tesla again is a wonderful example of how these components come together to create the self-driving experience. Their “Autopilot” system uses eight cameras to provide 360-degree visibility, while twelve ultrasonic sensors and a front-facing radar analyse

What is the likely impact?
One of the main impacts to come from the development of autonomous vehicles will be the increased safety when on the road. As autonomous vehicles can sense their environment, recognize pedestrians, traffic lanes, signage, and other vehicles, there will be a decline in driver-caused accidents. (Polly, 2020)
The use of autonomous vehicles will also help commuters to save time as the commute time can then be used for working, relaxing, reading, and accessing entertainment. (Ohio University, 2020)
Some of the predicted changes to come from autonomous vehicles being more readily available are that:

  1. People will be less likely to own a car (especially if they live in a city), as convenient driverless taxi/ride share services will be available in a few minutes. This will no longer be there (Bowman, 2018)
  2. Businesses will be able to utilise this technology when it is applied to vans and trucks. They can have a mini shop set up inside, maybe a gym, or even a portable office space. These services will be convenient for people that are either time poor, or people that have limited mobility.
  3. People that have limited mobility due to illness, old age, or disability will be able to live more independently as driverless shuttles will gain the ability to scan their surroundings with lasers, cameras, and radar to find the easiest place for a passenger to board.


While the introduction of autonomous vehicles will be great for the everyday commuter, the following sectors will see the impact from this technology the most

  1. Ridesharing/taxi services – with the vehicles becoming autonomous, the need for a driver will no longer be there (Bowman, 2018)
  2. Petrol stations and convenience stores within them – with more electric vehicles being on the road, less people will be fueling up with gas, petrol, or diesel. Unless the gas stations can change their models to include charging infrastructure, they will see a decline in customers. This will then flow onto the convenience stores within them as there will be less foot traffic (Bowman, 2018)
  3. Freight industry – self-driving trucks will in turn remove the need for truck drivers, leaving many without gainful employment and few prospects in different industries (Smith, 2018)

Machine Learning

What does it do?
Machine learning is the process of computer systems analysing large amounts of data to detect patterns so that it can effectively and accurately make predictions and perform tasks that it is not originally programmed to do so (Heath, 2020). Simply put, machine learning teaches computer systems to learn, like how humans learn based on experience. The data that machine learning algorithms analyse can range from text, numbers, images, sounds, and user input behaviour to name a few. Machine learning gives computer systems the ability to perform a very large variety of useful tasks that traditionally only humans were able to complete, ranging from recommending what song you may want to hear next, recognising speech patterns to correct typos and grammatical mistakes, and even detecting credit card fraud (Daffodil Software, 2017).

Machine learning can be divided into three major categories: supervised learning, unsupervised learning, and reinforcement learning. Supervised learning is the most predominant form of machine learning, where labelled data is used to train the machine learning algorithms to detects patterns and predict outcomes effectively (IBM Cloud Education, 2020). This works in a similar way to police dogs being trained to differentiate between illicit and non-illicit substances after being exposed to a large variety of examples. Unsupervised learning on the other hand tasks machine learning algorithms with detecting patterns on its own without the data itself being labelled. The algorithm will look for any connections in the data set and use this to split that data into appropriate categories (Burns, 2021). Finally, there is reinforcement machine learning, where the algorithm is fed instructions or guidelines and then makes its own decisions on how to handle tasks based on trial and error. The machine learning algorithm attempts many ways to reach its objective and is either rewarded or penalised based on whether its attempts help or get in the way of reaching the desired outcome (Hao, 2018). This can be compared to training a dog tricks by feeding the dog treats when it performs the desired task, or withholding treats when it does not. Reinforcement machine learning is what allowed Google’s program AlphaGo to beat human players in the game of Go (Byford, 2016).

Machine learning has many practical uses today, with virtual assistants such as Siri and the Google Assistant being great examples. Virtual assistants are applications that complete tasks for users by understanding natural voice commands. When a user makes a request for the virtual assistant, it sends this voice command to a server where not only the words are analysed by machine learning algorithms, but also the tone. The words and tone are matched to a request that the algorithm thinks you have asked, and then actions this request (Gupta, 2020). The machine learning algorithm keeps the requests that you have made so that any future voice commands can be compared. Over time the more one uses virtual assistants, the more natural language samples the algorithm has and the better it is at detecting voice patterns, tones, and accents, resulting in the virtual assistant learning to understand your voice with greater accuracy.

Machine learning has been advancing not only in the technology industries, but also the medical and pharmacology industries too. Machine learning assist with greater disease prediction by analysing not only typical variables such as age, height, weight, and gender, but also analyse studies that have been carried out and the demographics of patients to make a much more informed prediction for any pathologies a patient might experience (Zhydik, 2021). Drug research can also benefit from machine learning, by analysing datasets with a potential drug’s chemical structure to predict that drug’s impact on genes and cell lines to predict possible side effects. This will ultimately speed up the time it takes to test for a drug’s safety and accelerate the time it takes to bring that drug to the market.

What is the likely impact?
Developments that will change how much our lives will be impacted by machine learning include the adoption of quantum computing and robotics (Rose, 2020). Quantum computing utilises quantum mechanics to compute an extraordinarily large number of calculations in a much shorter time than classical computers (Corporation, 2021). Quantum computers are so powerful that if you to find one item in a list of one trillion items, and checking each item took 1 microsecond, it would take a classical computer approximately one week to execute this task, whereas it would take a quantum computer about 1 second (IBM, 2021). It’s important to remember that machine learning algorithms require a very large amount of data to be able to make predictions accurately, so utilising the power of quantum computers means that computer systems will be able to make much more accurate predictions and estimates in a much faster time frame than if we were to rely solely on classical computers.

As machine learning develops even further in the future, it is expected that robotics will benefit from this and become responsible for even more business activities (Rose, 2020). Robots will be able to utilise machine learning algorithms to perform tasks in a faster manner and with far greater accuracy than humans, such as analysing customer behaviours and insights. Jobs that are likely to be impacted by the rise of machine learning and robotics include business analyst, hedge fund managers, and lawyers just to name a few (Uzialko, 2019). It isn’t expected that this developing technology will one day replace thousands of jobs for people working in these roles, however it means that it is going to become increasingly harder to find these jobs over time.

Arduinos and other Small Computing Devices

What are they?
Arduinos and an open-source electronics platform designed to be accessible and to help people learn about programming and how it can manipulate computers to perform specific tasks. Arduinos are sold as an individual minicomputer or in a bundle which will come with different add-on modules such as, motors, LEDs, and buttons. The Arduino is controlled by sending messages to the microcontroller on the Arduino board. These messages are sent via the Arduino programming language and the Arduino Software which is an IDE (Arduino – Introduction, 2021).

Arduinos can be used for a various number of small projects designed towards beginners, but also has the complexity to be used for larger projects and is used by student, hobbyists, professionals, and programmers. The open-source nature of the platform allows for its users to expand upon what can be done with the devices and a library of extensive knowledge has been built which will continue to grow as people keep using the Arduino platform.

Arduinos are extremely accessible as they are inexpensive, multi-platform, open-source, and simple. According to Arduino’s website they sell pre-assembled Arduino modules starting at $50 and even cheaper options if you wish to assemble the Arduino by hand. The Arduino IDE runs on Windows, MacOS, and Linus operating systems. Alternatives to Arduino are mostly limited to only Windows.

What do they do?
Arduinos can read inputs and generate outputs. Relatively simple but can be used to achieve big results. Inputs such as a light senser, a button, a microphone, and even messages in social media. Through the Arduino programming language and the Arduino software, these inputs can trigger outputs such as turning on a motor, a light, or even posting onto social media. These are just simple inputs and outputs, but they pave the way to more complicated projects which can be fun and even solve problems through innovation. Some of the fun projects people have made with Arduino include, an Arduino Piano, a fingerprint lock, gaming consoles, and small robots (20 Awesome Arduino Projects That You Must Try 2021!, 2021).

Most of the projects made with Arduino are for fun and for learning, but there are cases of Arduinos being used to solve real problems and automating processes used in the workplace. People have been using Arduino and other brands of small computing devices to create automated irrigation devices, although on a small scale only, specifically in home greenhouses or small vegetable gardens, advancements like this have potential to develop into large scale improvements. Unfortunately, due to the limited processing power and RAM on a small computing device, large scale projects would require either many small computing devices or a more powerful solution. This limitation is what keeps Arduinos and other small computing devices from rapid development. The most common Arduino, the Arduino Duo boasts 16MHz of processing power and 2kb of SRAM which compared to a modern-day phone is insignificant. This limitation to computing power is why Arduinos succeed is automation of smaller tasks as they require minimal processing power, and the low cost of the device makes the process of automation more accessible.

Moore’s law states that the number of transistors doubles approximately every two years in technology. With the growth of technology each year we are able to get smaller or more powerful microprocessors. In the year 2018 a microprocessor was produced which was 0.04mm3, smaller than a grain of rice. The device was wireless and even didn’t require a battery to operate. To transmit data the device used visible light. (Microcontroller - Wikipedia, 2021).

Students who enjoy hands on experience with microcontrollers such as Arduino might gain an understanding and even passion for computer engineering and due to the accessibility of Arduino its being used by schools to teach about robotics and programming in a fun way at an early age. Almost everything we use today has some sort of motherboard or microcontroller. From your kettle to your remote controller, if you press a button and it sends an output there is a high chance your device uses a microcontroller. In 3 years, as the technology improves at a rate of 100% every two years according to Moore’s law, we will see microcontrollers that are twice as powerful as the ones we have today and for the ones we do have today, they will be cheaper and more accessible for small businesses, students and hobbyist alike, and with more people using and developing with them innovation will occur.

Cybersecurity

What Does it Do?
‘Cyber-crime has been on the rise for years now and it is not showing any signs of slowing down (Meharchandani, 2021)’. With information and communication technologies being such a large part of our daily lives, it becomes more important than ever to protect oneself from the external risks that the digital age presents. ‘In Australia, the term 'cybercrime' is used to describe both crimes directed at computers or other information communication technologies (hereafter known as ICTs) and crimes where computers or ICTs are an integral part of an offence (Australian Federal Police, n.d).

Naturally, the antithesis to cyber-crime is cybersecurity. Defined as ‘the practice of protecting critical systems and sensitive information from digital attacks (IBM n.d),’ cybersecurity was developed as the result of instances like the 1988 worm created by Robert T. Morris; a graduate from Cornwell University; whose worm replicated so aggressively it clogged networks, caused systems to crash and inadvertently led to the rise of the computer virus.

With the rise of virus’; regardless of the creator’s intentions; came the rise of antivirus software solutions. Early iterations of antivirus solutions scanned ‘IT systems and tested them with signatures written within a database (Mutune 2021)’ but, by 2007 these earlier solutions had become antiquated and could no longer provide adequate protection from the ever-growing number of malware samples and nowadays, it is not enough to simply have a strong password, and even the strongest of password policies are susceptible to cyber-attacks. Instead, it is important to have a good cybersecurity system that consists of multi-layered strategies that protect against cyber-crime.

For example the implentation of an Identity and Access Management Systems; is ‘about defining and managing the roles and access privileges of individual network entities (Storm 2021) and its aim is simple; one digital identity per end user that can be used across multiple devices and networks. It achieves this by using tools such as Single Sign-On and Multi-Factor Authentication which provide an end user with the ability to ‘log into a network once without re-entering credentials (IBM n.d). A typical identity and access management system is made up of four elements; a directory which is used to identify users within the system, the ability to add, modify and delete user data as organisational and personal requirements change, a system that controls user access and an audit that reports on user access.

Additionally, an organisation could implement a Security Information and Event Management system (or SEIM) such as Splunk Enterprise Security and IBM’s QRadar. First appearing in 2005 as the successor to Security Information Management and Security Event Management which had previously been separate entities, these systems help to detect suspicious activity by utilising artificial intelligence to analyse end user behaviour. In attempting to do so, a typical Security Information and Event Management system follows the following four steps

  1. Collection of data
  2. Normalisation and grouping of data
  3. Analysis of dta
  4. Diagnosis of security threats

However, these technological innovations have been around for quite some time; what about the latest in cybersecurity technology and what trends should be expected because of innovation within the cybersecurity space?

The integration of Artificial Intelligence has already been seen within cybersecurity, Adaptive Networks are relatively new within cybersecurity and ‘remakes a conventional network into a programmable infrastructure (Preimesberger 2018). An adaptive network uses analytics, automation, and machine learning to guide policies and allows the infrastructure within the network to evolve into an application programming interface (or API) instead of being a standalone entity. As a result of the automation of network tasks, such as network monitoring and policy and compliance validation; errors within the network that result from human error are minimised, allowing the network to be more responsive and anticipate bandwidth requirements and potential threats both within an organisation and externally.

Another evolving tool within cybersecurity is Blockchain Cybersecurity. IBM defines Blockchain as the ‘shared, immutable ledger that facilitates the process of recording transactions and tracking assets in a business network (IBM n.d). When used in a decentralized manner the network is owned by a collective group which is made up of multiple computers known as nodes, rather than a single entity. This means that a hacker cannot gain access to user data by simply exploiting a single vulnerability within a system but instead, creates a near impenetrable wall that cannot easily be accessed. Further on this point, because Blockchain technology is immutable it means that data cannot easily be changed, and transactions sent within the network or encrypted data sent to other networks are safe from tampering.

What is the Likely Impact?
Cybersecurity tools implemented in today's day and age, or even those in the future are impervious to attacks, or failure. Cybersecurity affects everyone; from the newborn child whose parents have inevitably posted their photo and full name on social media, to the couple that has recently implemented smart technology throughout their new home, or the middle-aged mums sharing memes on Facebook or even the 90-year-old grandparent learning to use technology so that they can stay connected with their loved ones. Everyone is a potential target, and the introduction of cybersecurity and cybercrime will affect different people in diverse ways. For instance, an elderly technology user may be more susceptible to scams and financial threats whilst on the other end of the spectrum, a teen or young adult may be more susceptible to cyberbullying and grooming. The main takeaway is that it is important that each person is educated on the risks of cybercrime.

At the end of the day, social engineering indicates that human error is the biggest vulnerability within the cybersecurity space and can be divided into two main categories: skilled based and decision-based errors. A skill-based error relates to an error that has occurred because an end user has completed a task incorrectly. On the other hand, a decision-based error refers to a mistake in judgement and can further be broken down to rule-based errors and knowledgebased errors. The latter refers to an instance where an end user does not have the knowledge required to perform an action, whilst the former (rule-based errors) ‘relies on basic human behaviour psychology and neuroscience (Babati n.d)’ to determine patterns in behaviour and exploit these patterns; the most common of which are quizzes that ask user’s specific password recovery questions such as their first pet, or favourite food.

How Will This Affect You?
What can be done to protect myself and my loved ones? The most drastic action would be to remove technology which, in today's day and age is near impossible. Instead, the best action is education and awareness; this can be done both in a user’s personal life and in the workplace and can include training on the risks of cybercrime, training employees to recognise phishing and social engineering attempts and ‘conducting “Live Fire” practice attacks (Jones n.d)’ which simulate attacks like a fire drill
